EFFECTIVE READING INSTRUCTION

What are the 5 elements of an effective reading instruction program for elementary readers?

Put Reading First: The Research Building Blocks for Teaching Children to Read*
is a guide for teachers that summarizes what researchers have discovered about how to successfully teach children to read. It describes the findings of the National Reading Panel Report (2000) and provides analysis and discussion in the five areas of reading instruction:

1. Phonemic Awareness
2. Phonics
3. Fluency
4. Vocabulary
5. Comprehension

*This report, in its entirety, can be found at:
Put Reading First: The Research Building Blocks for Teaching Children to Read
http://lincs.ed.gov/publications/pdf/PRFbooklet.pdf

1. Phonemic Awareness - hear, identify and manipulate sounds; the understanding that the sounds of spoken language work together to make words.

Phonemic awareness is a subcategory of phonological awareness. Phonological awareness includes identifying and manipulating larger parts of spoken language, such as words, syllables, and also other aspects of sound, such as rhyming.

Phonics /Alphabet Recognition/Alphabetic Principal
In order for reading to occur, students must have alphabet recognition.
Regardless of the label, the goal of phonics instruction is to help children learn and use the alphabetic principle--the understanding that there are systematic and predictable relationships between written letters and spoken sounds. Knowing these relationships will help children recognize familiar words accurately and automatically, and "decode" new words. In short, knowledge of the alphabetic principle contributes greatly to children's ability to read words both in isolation and in connected text.


Students must understand that letters have distinct sounds. For alphabetic principle, students must be able to visually recognize and name the letters of the alphabet in both lower and uppercase. High Frequency Words / Sight Words

"Only 100 words account for 50% of all words in English print." (Fry 1993)
Sight words are an important part of reading instruction and word study. Strong knowledge of sight words improves readers' fluency and aids comprehension. One way to help your students identify words is to practice. This technique is especially helpful with emergent readers who have trouble reading because they can't identify enough words to read a book. This list of 300 high-frequency words can be used to develop activities aimed at boosting students' sight word recognition.

SECONDARY READING INSTRUCTION includes the same five, plus one more. . . . . . . Morphology.

The element of reading specific to older students is the concept of Morphology.

Morphology is the study of word structure. It describes how words are formed from morphemes, the smallest unit of meaning in a word. These units of meaning could be roots, prefixes and suffixes. Students who understand words and their morphological structure are better able to get the meaning of words and are better prepared for increased reading and writing demands across the curriculum in different content areas.

Students with knowledge of morphology are better able to separate out the morphemes into meaningful units which can be used in decoding and comprehending unfamiliar words.
