Review the following side by side comparison of Narrative and Expository Texts.

Narrative TextExpository Text

Invites reader into the story through characters to which reader can relate. Invites reader into text through explaining information but has no characters or situations that draw the reader into the selection.

Now that you know the difference between narrative and expository texts, let's take a closer look at the two types of text.

Narrative Text
First we are going to examine Narrative Text. Go back to your handouts and read the story, The Dinner Party. Then, complete the Narrative Frame handout.

Narrative frames require students to identify the most important/crucial information in a story (main idea), the information that students MUST BE ABLE TO RECALL.

Expository Text
Now we are going to focus on Expository text.
Up to about the third grade most of the material that students are asked to read or that is read to them is narrative. Therefore, when students progress and are expected to read expository text some have difficulty transferring strategies used in reading one type of text to the other. Students have to learn on their own or be taught how to apply reading strategies to the expository text found in science, history, math and other content areas.

Locate your handout on Fluency Instruction and the K-W-L Chart. Read the Fluency article and complete the K-W-L chart.

The K-W-L chart is an instructional technique that is used to activate students' prior knowledge by asking them what they already Know; then students (as a class or within small groups) set goals specifying what they Want to learn; and after reading students discuss what they have Learned. The three parts of this procedure - brainstorming, establishing purposes through questioning and finding answers to those questions, almost guarantee that students will be actively involved in learning.

The two activities you have just completed, the Narrative Frame and the KWL Chart, are similar to what you can use to help your students organize information they read in your classroom.

Text Factors affect the reading of Expository Text.
Considering that most reading required of secondary students is of expository text, it is important that students know and understand how information is structured and presented in such text. Knowing this will allow students to utilize appropriate strategies in identifying important information within the text. Failure to identify such information is a major cause of low comprehension when reading expository text. You as teachers need to know about the different types of expository text in order to alert students about the structure of a paragraph which can dictate what type of information is contained within it.
