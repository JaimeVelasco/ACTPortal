How can I prepare my students for these tests?

The State of Texas Assessments of Academic Readiness (STAAR) is a new state assessment for students in grades 3-12. STAAR replaced the Texas Assessment of Knowledge and Skills (TAKS) in the spring of 2012. The STAAR program includes annual assessments for grades 3-8 in reading and mathematics; assessments in writing at grades 4 and 7; in science at grades 5 and 8; and in social studies at grade 8; and end-of-course assessments for high school students.

We will be referencing and practicing with the STAAR in this module. Although testing programs change, the process of test analysis, benchmark information and intervention strategies presented in this module are state mandated training requirements.

The STAAR testing program has serious implications for you as a teacher especially at the high school level. It is important that you have a general understanding of the STAAR testing program. Read through "STAAR FAQ" provided on the TEA website to help provide clarity to this complex process!

Background Information on STAAR
The State of Texas Assessments of Academic Readiness (STAAR) is the standardized testing program used in Texas primary and secondary schools to assess students' attainment of reading, writing, math, science, and social studies skills required under Texas education standards. Legislation requires states to measure student progress in reading and math annually in grades 3 - 8 and at least once in grades 10 - 12. In Texas, students must meet the standard on STAAR to graduate from high school. Results from the STAAR tests are also used to determine the ratings for districts and campuses in the state of Texas.

The STAAR test is aligned to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS), state-mandated curriculum to determine the objectives to assess on each grade level.

The tested subjects vary depending by grade level; however, reading and math are always part of the exam. Every portion of the exam includes multiple choice questions. The high school reading and English language arts (combined reading and writing) tests also require students to answer open-ended short answer questions on a narrative and expository selection. The writing and ELA tests include a written composition as well. Unlike the TAKS, the STAAR is timed.

TEA provides STAAR Information Booklets containing details, student expectations, rubrics, and sample test items. Some released tests are also available online.

High school students will take STAAR End-of-Course (EOC) tests throughout their years in high school. The required end-of-course assessments include English I, English II, Algebra I, Biology and U.S. History.

The STAAR EOC tests focus solely on the curriculm covered in that course. In contrast, the TAKS measured overall knowlege of the general subject, along with some specific questions from course curriculum.

Texas high school seniors cannot graduate unless they pass exit-level STAAR tests in English language arts, social studies, math, and science. During their junior and senior years of high school, students are given five chances to pass the test.

There are alternate Assessments for students receiving special education services. Those students being home-schooled or attending private schools are not required to take the STAAR test.

STAAR Analysis

As a teacher, you may obtain test information from previous years on all of your students. You will be required to do so in some schools.
Why analyze the test results? The benefits of analyzing test results are two-fold. . . .

      1. It empowers students by knowing their own strengths and weaknesses so that they can set goals towards improvement.
      2. Teachers benefit by determining what skills or objectives to focus on or re-teach.

Test analysis is almost always required of STAAR and benchmark tests so that you can check the progression of your students throughout the school year.

Benchmarks and Interventions
Standards-based assessments are sometimes called benchmarks because they are used to gather data on how students are progressing in their mastery of the curriculum. In Texas, the standards are the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS). The TEKS are aligned through the grades so students in Texas schools are mastering a curriculum that builds as they progress through the grades. The TEKS are also aligned with national standards and College Board expectations.

Benchmarks are "district" assessments, which means that every student in that course or grade level in the district takes the same test at the same time. Data from standards-based benchmarks can be disaggregated according to specific standards rather than just reported as an overall grade like many classroom assessments.

Benchmark tests are used to measure the achievement of important standards at intervals throughout the school year. These benchmarks inform teachers of the progress that each of their students is making toward the achievement of crucial standards likely to be assessed on annual standards-based tests such as STAAR.

Research shows a strong predictive relationship between students' performance on benchmark tests and their performance on state assessments.

Not only are Benchmark and STAAR scores referred to by teachers, they are also looked at by campuses and at the district level. School wide scores are reported to principals, and teachers are to develop intervention and remediation plans for students. Teachers are also expected to re-teach skills according to student performance on these tests.

Teachers are to look for student gains from year to year. In most districts, this information is available online, and it is the teacher's responsibility to keep track of his/her student data and be sure that students are recommended for appropriate interventions outside the classroom if needed (tutoring, special programs, etc.). There are also district interventions, such as special reading or math classes that are required for students not passing the STAAR test.

DOCUMENT, DOCUMENT, DOCUMENT. . . . Your administrators will base our job performance by how well the kids do on benchmarks and STAAR! Documentation of student progress is a must!

Helping students progress on STAAR can help you keep your job. You should chart student progress on benchmarks and STAAR tests. This information should be readily available to administrators and Field Supervisors.
