Language Experience Approach

Rationale for LEA - The LEA supports children's concept development and vocabulary growth while offering many opportunities for meaningful reading and writing activities. As such, students, through the LEA, will be able to demonstrate emerging:

  • awareness that print conveys meaning
  • awareness that everyone's thoughts and ideas can be expressed in spoken and written language
  • ability to gather meaning from reading or listening to others read resources that relate to personal experiences
  • ability to make meaning known by speaking in informal situations
  • ability to use listening to understand the meaning and intent of others
  • ability to tell and retell personal experiences
  • ability to dictate stories, sentences, and phrases for others to transcribe
  • ability to understand:

    WHAT I CAN SAY, SOMEONE CAN WRITE DOWN
    WHAT IS WRITTEN DOWN THAT I SAID, CAN BE READ OR SAID

Procedure - The procedure for the LEA can involve the whole class or a small group.

  1. Choose a focus - a wide range of possibilities exist for the creation of language experience reading and writing. These include:
  • growing vegetables or flowers in the classroom (Making a "How to Grow Vegetables/Flowers" chart)
  • planning a field trip, taking and writing about it both before and afterward (making lists of things to bring, rules to follow; drawing and labeling a map of where you went; describing the experience)
  • making various kinds of "how to" charts describing experiments the class has done or things they have made
  • making a record of a cultural event such as a Pow-Wow or of the visit of a special guest
  • summarizing a favorite story that children have heard, viewed, or dramatized several times
  • Note: Sometimes occasions for using the LEA arise spontaneously such as an early thunderstorm, experiments with mixing paint, events that capture an event or experience that creates a lot of interest or captures children's imaginations
2. Demonstrate print concepts as you record the experience. Initially in language experience, it is the teacher or another adult who does the recording (writing on the chart tablet). This is because the approach is intended to demonstrate to children the match between what they say and its written form, and to develop other print concepts. The teacher would draw attention to these aspects of print while writing on chart paper positioned so as to be visible to all the children.
3. Record children's own language. The most important aspect of recording is to use children's own words, keeping the match between what they say and what you write. Use children's names as much as possible because their own name is one of the first words children learn to recognize. As well, this practice helps to maintain children's connections to the experience in subsequent reading.
  4. Vary the type of record that you create. The written product of your experience might be made into a big book, a bulletin board, or an illustrated chart. Other ideas for creating a lasting record of the experience include the following:
  Create a class album through using your classroom camera to capture an experience. Children can dictate captions for each photograph and you can discuss concepts of print as you record what they dictate.
 

Have children work in pairs to draw a picture of their favorite part of the experience then have them dictate the accompanying text for the teacher to write on a sentence strip. These pictures and sentence strips can then be used in a sequencing activity ("What did we do first? next? etc.") and made into a bulletin board or Big Book to be used for Shared Reading.

Note: Remember, you cannot record a statement from every child every time you create an LEA record. Stop the discussion and recording before the children lose interest. Pay attention to who has not had an opportunity and include them when you complete this record later in the day, the following day, or the next time you use this approach.

  5. Use your records of the experience for further literacy opportunities. A main purpose of the language experience approach is to provide meaningful texts for students to read either with the support of others or alone. To facilitate this, the record of the experience should be mounted in a prominent place for several weeks following its creation and referred to regularly. Some of the ways the chart, Big Book, etc. can be used include:
  Shared Reading - A "Framing" activity that will help children develop the concepts of word and sentence as well as left to right progression.
  Independent and "Take Home" Reading - Making a copy of the record to be photocopied for each child to illustrate, use for independent and buddy reading, and to take home to read to their families.
  Sentence-matching - making large sentence strips that replicate sentences in the record for a sentence matching activity; students can attempt to read the sentence during Shared Reading. Invite volunteers to find and place each sentence strip over the one that matches it in the experience chart or record.
  Sequencing Words Into Sentences - following sentence matching activities, sentence strips from the class chart or record can also be cut up into words. Demonstrate this but allow the children to do the cutting. They can work individually or in pairs to reconstruct the sentences.
  Child-led Rereading Activities - inviting students to become the teacher and lead other children through parts of the chart, Big, Book, etc. by pointing out words they know, or reading aloud specific sections and framing them by sweeping their hand or a pointer along as they read. As you develop many charts and books using the LEA, children can choose which chart or book they would like to lead the other students in rereading.

 

Guided Reading

Follow along with your instructor they go through the principles, benefits and characteristics of Guided Reading.

Guided Reading is a valuable strategy for any level student. As we have discussed earlier, textbooks used in subject areas often include language, vocabulary and concepts specific to a particular field of study. We can't just assign reading. Teachers must help prepare students for and guide them through the texts so that they will learn most effectively.

Read Aloud
A Read Aloud is a planned oral reading of a book or print usually related to a theme or topic of study. It can be used to model the pleasure of reading and the use of reading strategies and aide in comprehension by motivating students and activating prior knowledge.


Shared Reading
In Shared Reading, the teacher reads the book aloud on more than one occasion. The first time for enjoyment, the second reading might focus on building and extending comprehension through interaction with students.

Although Leslie's examples are for an elementary class, visuals are for everyone. Think about what catches your eye. Your students are most definitely attracted to visuals, whether it's to introduce or to teach a topic.

SSR
Sustained Silent Reading - SSR has many forms and names, but generally it is a fixed time period for silent reading during class. It usually requires at least 20 minutes a day in which students read self-selected material silently with no monitoring or other classroom assignments.

Here are some other names for SSR:

USSR - Uninterrupted Sustained Silent Reading
DEAR - Drop Everything and Read
FUR - Free Uninterrupted Reading
SQUIRT - Sustained Quiet Uninterrupted Independent Reading
