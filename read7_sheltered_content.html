EFFECTIVE READING INSTRUCTION

Activity 7 - Sheltered Content

What is sheltered content instruction and how does it help readers?

Sheltered content instruction or the SIOP (Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol) model, are recommendations that were developed over the course of a seven year research project specifically designed to promote English learners' academic literacy in content areas. Although these strategies were developed for the English language learner or ESL (English as a Second Language) student, any student can benefit from these techniques.

  • Identify the language demands of the content
  • Plan language objectives for all lessons, and make them explicit to students
  • Emphasize academic vocabulary development
  • Activate and strengthen background or prior knowledge
  • Promote oral interaction and extended academic discussion
  • Review vocabulary and content concepts
  • Give students feedback on language/vocabulary use in class


As we have already determined in a previous activity, there is a major difference between narrative and expository texts. The main difference is in the types of language being used. The difference is especially important when students come from a non-English background.

Read the following passage about Two Types of Language Proficiency.

THERE ARE TWO TYPES OF LANGUAGE you are going to have to deal with: One (BICS), You will use with your students as people in normal conversation; the other (CALP) You will use with your students as students for teaching and directions.

Requirements of TWO TYPES OF LANGUAGE PROFICIENCY:

1. BICS (BASIC INTERPERSONAL COMMUNICATION SKILLS)
[often called CONVERSATIONAL LANGUAGE]
BICS / CONVERSATIONAL LANGUAGE =
  • philosophical, religious, or political conversations
  • use of conversational skills
  • use of correct grammar
  • vocabulary development across useful conversation areas
  • use of sentences
  • adequate social communication
  • use of phrases, even one- and two- word phrases
  • receptive/and expressive use of language

2. CALP (COGNITIVE-ACADEMIC LANGUAGE PROFICIENCY)
[often called ACADEMIC LANGUAGE]
  • grade-level literacy
  • study skills and advanced reading skills
  • reading and writing fluency
  • expository writing
  • expository reading
  • reading comprehension skills
  • narrative composition
  • narrative reading
  • school skills: following directions, cooperation, listening

So. . . . what's the point????

Many of the textbooks that you will be using will be at different levels of difficulty and you must be prepared to use strategies to assist your students to tackle difficult texts. These strategies are good for all students that struggle, not just ESL students.

Sheltered content instruction benefits all learners because... talking about, reading about, and writing about content in different subject areas challenges every student's English as most vocabulary, phrases, and ideas expressed are unique to the subject under study; therefore, content area reading is English as a second language for the greater majority of students no matter what their native language.

What are effective techniques to make difficult texts easily comprehensible?

Some people believe Effective Instruction works best, others believe that Sheltered Content Instruction works best. Ideally, instruction should include a combination of both types.

Effective techniques and procedures to make difficult texts easily comprehensible:

In session #3 we talked about two types of text and you were given examples of graphic organizers that can be used to help students organize information from both types of text.

Using graphic organizers such as story maps and webs support higher level thinking and comprehension by providing a framework for focusing on important points, rather than getting lost in unimportant details.

Graphic organizers and discussion help students think about and organize ideas. The primary purpose for engaging students in any of the procedures is to enhance their reading enjoyment, to foster understanding and learning and to increase their competence and confidence in making meaning with texts.

For more information and examples of graphic organizers: What Content-Area Teachers Should Know About Adolescent Literacy http://lincs.ed.gov/publications/pdf/adolescent_literacy07.pdf (pages 50-61)
